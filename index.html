Track 04 · New for 2026

The marathon.

A long-form endurance test for navigation policies. One long-distance run strung together from mixed legs across urban arterials, off-road terrain, and indoor handoffs — single policy end-to-end.

01 · Format

One long run · seven legs

The run chains together urban, off-road and indoor segments. No driver hand-offs, no policy swaps mid-run. The route probes whether one model can hold up across domain shifts and accumulate kilometers without drift.

02 · Route

UC Berkeley → Stanford

~50 mi over the Bay Area corridor, split into seven named legs (1.1, 1.2, 2.1, 2.2, 3.1, 3.1.5, 3.2). Mix of city sidewalks, bay-side bike paths, overpasses, and suburban arterials. The flyover at left traces the planned path.

03 · Why it matters

Generalization, under load

Single-domain benchmarks don't reveal whether a policy can handle the seams. The marathon stresses long-horizon recovery, scene-shift robustness, and the cumulative cost of small mistakes — the failure modes researchers most often miss in sim.

04 · Scoring

Per-leg difficulty · TBD

Leg-level scoring formula being finalized with FrodoBots, GMU and NYU. Expected to follow the same 1–10 difficulty rubric as the other three tracks, with cumulative time as the tiebreaker.
Track 01 · Flagship

Urban navigation.

Outdoor city sidewalks across multiple cities worldwide. Goals are GPS waypoints; the rover must reach within 15 m of each checkpoint, in real traffic, with real pedestrians, on real sidewalks.

01 · Goal

GPS waypoints · 15 m tolerance

Each mission is a sequence of GPS checkpoints. A checkpoint counts as reached when the rover comes within ~15 m — the tolerance accounts for GPS noise on consumer hardware in dense urban canyons.

02 · Real-world variability

Same fleet, different cities

Sites span 10+ cities globally — Botswana, Mauritius, Kenya, Nigeria, Mexico, Costa Rica, Panama, the US. Sidewalk widths, curb cuts, crossing geometry, traffic mix, lighting and weather all vary. The policy that wins is the one that generalizes.

03 · Difficulty rubric

Rated 1–10 per mission

Difficulty is set per mission based on road crossings, pedestrian and traffic complexity, expected drive time, number of turns, and path type — parks and rural roads are easier; bike paths, sidewalks and on-road segments scale up.
Track 02 · Indoor

Indoor navigation.

No GPS, no map. Hallways, atriums, labs — the rover must reach an image-defined goal using only its camera stream.

01 · Goal

Image-goal driven

Each mission specifies the destination as a target image. The rover is judged on whether it can reach a viewpoint that matches that image, navigating only from the live camera feed.

02 · Environment

Hallways, atriums, labs

Indoor corridors, doorways, open atriums and cluttered lab space. Glass walls, repeating textures, and reflections all break naïve visual localization — the policy has to be robust to scene-level ambiguity.

03 · Difficulty rubric

Rated 1–10 per mission

Difficulty scales with the number of turns, scene-shift between rooms, lighting changes, and the visual distance between the goal image and the rover's starting view.
Track 03 · Off-road

Off-road terrain.

Trails, slopes, gravel, unstructured natural terrain. Image-goal driven — no GPS — over surfaces the rover was never shaped for.

01 · Goal

Image-goal driven

Same as indoor: missions specify the destination as a target image. The rover navigates from camera input alone, with no GPS waypoints to fall back on.

02 · Terrain

Trails, slopes, gravel

Unstructured natural terrain — uneven ground, varied traction, and lighting that shifts with canopy and time of day. Visual cues are sparser than in urban settings, and self-similar at a distance.

03 · Difficulty rubric

Rated 1–10 per mission

Difficulty scales with slope, traction loss, scene self-similarity, and the visual distance between goal image and starting view. Cumulative time on shared missions is the tiebreaker.
